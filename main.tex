\begin{abstract}
  Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations (MERC) aims to recognize human emotions for each utterance via visual, audio, and linguistic modalities. Its potential uses in affective computing and human-computer interaction have drawn a lot of attention in recent years. Among the various approaches proposed to solve this problem, graph based approaches have emerged as some of the most promising ones. However, because this pixel level feature extraction is noisy , the visual modality frequently makes only a small contribution or is underutilized in many graph based MERC frameworks. Traditional visual encoders generally focus on geometric and textual patterns to produce feature maps or patch representations. These representations often fail to sufficiently express the facial and behavioral cues leading to weak cross modal alignment with the other semantically rich modalities. To address this problem, we propose a Vision Language Model (VLM) based feature extraction approach that captures facial expressions and behavioral cues from the visual modality and encodes them as semantically meaningful representations. Using this approach, the raw video for each utterance is translated into a descriptive and meaningful textual representation through a VLM with structured prompts designed to capture facial expressions, behavioral cues, and body language. The resulting semantic visual features are then incorporated into graph based MERC frameworks as a replacement for low level visual features, reducing noise in the visual modality while increasing the model’s reliance on meaningful visual information through the VLM. This work presents VLM-based semantic visual feature extraction as a viable replacement for conventional visual feature extraction pipelines in graph-based MERC frameworks.

  \keywords{Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations \and Vision Language Models \and Semantic Visual Features \and Graph Neural Networks}
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The proper interpretation and communication of emotions are crucial, as they significantly influence interactions, conversations, and decision making processes. As this modern world advances to an AI dependent future it has become essential to ensure that these emerging AI systems and computers are capable of recognizing emotions at a level comparable to, or exceeding, human ability. But so far understanding human emotions in conversations has been a fundamental challenge in human–computer interaction and affective computing \cite{calvo2010affect}.
Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations (MERC) aims to identify the emotion expressed in each utterance by jointly leveraging information from multiple modalities, typically textual, acoustic, and visual signals \cite{poria2019meld,zadeh2018multimodal}. Unlike traditional single utterance emotion recognition, MERC must consider contextual dependencies across speakers and time, making it a more complex and realistic setting \cite{majumder2019dialoguernn,ghosal2019dialoguegcn}. Over the past years, numerous approaches have been adopted to solve this problem, starting from early fusion based techniques and transformer driven multimodal interaction models to more structured and context aware graph based architectures \cite{zadeh2017tfn,tsai2019mult,ghosal2019dialoguegcn,hu2021mmgcn,shou2025comprehensivesurveymultimodalconversational}.
Recent advances have demonstrated that the graph based architectures, particularly those inspired by Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) \cite{kipf2017gcn}, are highly effective for MERC \cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn,hu2021mmgcn}. By modeling conversational structure as a graph, where utterances are treated as nodes and contextual relationships as edges, these approaches capture both inter speaker and intra speaker dependencies \cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn}. As a result, graph based MERC models achieve competitive performance on benchmark datasets and are considered among the most promising approaches for conversational emotion recognition \cite{hu2021mmgcn}.

Despite these advances these graph based approaches still face significant problems. One of them is that the visual modality often remains underutilized in many graph based MERC frameworks \cite{hu2021mmgcn,li2024graphsmile}. Traditional visual encoders, typically based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) \cite{he2016resnet} or vision transformers (ViTs) \cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}, extract low level geometric, texture based, or patch level representations from raw video frames. While effective for general visual recognition tasks, these representations often fail to capture subtle behavioral cues and body language that are crucial for emotion understanding \cite{li2018self}. Consequently, the visual modality may contribute less than textual or acoustic modalities, which naturally encode higher level semantic information \cite{hazarika2018icon}.

Despite advancements, graph based Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversation (MERC) approaches still face significant limitations , one of them being underutilization of a specific modality. Most of the currently existing graph based MERC frameworks are prone to this limitation underutilizing visual modality while giving priority to the textual modality \cite{hu2021mmgcn,li2024graphsmile}.

Traditional visual encoders relied upon in these baselines, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) \cite{he2016resnet} or Vision Transformers (ViTs) \cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}, are fundamentally designed to extract abstract spatial features, structural hierarchies, or patch level representations from isolated, static video frames. While adequate for general object recognition, these representations create a semantic gap, failing to explicitly capture the subtle behavioral cues, interaction with the other characters in the scene and dynamic body language necessary for complex emotion understanding \cite{li2018self}. Moreover, in standard MERC pipelines, the temporal evolution of emotion within an utterance is largely disregarded. Because graph networks require a fixed size node representation for structural modeling
\cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn, hu2021mmgcn}, frame level visual embeddings are typically aggregated into utterance level representations via statistical pooling operations, as adopted in multimodal benchmarks \cite{zadeh2018multimodal}. This aggregation effectively collapses the intra utterance temporal dimension. As a result, dynamic behavioral shifts (such as a speaker transitioning from a frustrated frown to a resigned smile) are mathematically averaged into a single static and potentially emotionally ambiguous feature vector \cite{tsai2019mult}.

Furthermore, real world conversational videos are prone to noise, including illumination variations, occlusions, and background clutter. This noise severely degrades the quality of pixel level features. Consequently, the visual modality tends to contribute less to emotion understanding compared to textual or acoustic modalities, which inherently encode higher level semantic information. This modality imbalance leads to weak cross modal alignment in multimodal fusion. Textual features derived from pretrained language models encode high level semantics \cite{devlin2019bert}, and acoustic features capture prosodic patterns associated with affect \cite{schuller2011recognising}. In contrast, low level visual embeddings remain largely appearance based, limiting effective multimodal integration, particularly in graph based architectures where node representations critically influence relational reasoning \cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn}.



To address this limitation, we propose rethinking visual feature extraction for graph based MERC by moving from abstract spatial embeddings to contextualized, semantically meaningful visual descriptions. Specifically, we leverage advanced Vision Language Models, specifically VidEmo-3B (built upon the Qwen2.5-VL-3B architecture \cite{qwen2_5_vl}), which are highly capable of interpreting dynamic visual content and generating temporally grounded semantic representations. Instead of directly feeding mean-pooled, emotionally ambiguous visual vectors into the graph network, we translate each utterance level video segment into a temporally structured textual narrative. This narrative explicitly tracks the evolution of facial expressions, head movements, and body language from the beginning to the end of the utterance, preserving the critical intra utterance dynamics that traditional pipelines can not retain.

Through carefully designed prompts, the VLM extracts affect relevant visual information and encodes it into descriptive semantic representations. These generated textual features serve as high level visual summaries that are better aligned with linguistic and acoustic modalities \cite{radford2021clip}. The resulting semantic visual features are then integrated into graph based MERC architectures, replacing traditional low level visual embeddings. By doing so, we reduce visual noise, improve cross modal consistency, and encourage the model to rely on meaningful visual signals rather than appearance based artifacts.

In summary, this work makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item We identify and analyze the underutilization of the visual modality in graph based MERC frameworks due to noisy and low level feature representations.
  \item We propose a VLM based semantic visual feature extraction approach that transforms raw video into structured textual descriptions capturing emotion relevant cues.
  \item We integrate these semantic visual features into graph based MERC architectures, promoting stronger cross modal alignment.
\end{enumerate}

By bridging the gap between visual perception and semantic understanding, our approach offers a new direction for enhancing the role of visual information in multimodal conversational emotion recognition.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Emotion Recognition in Conversations and Multimodal Extensions}

Emotion Recognition in Conversations (ERC) aims to identify the emotional state of each utterance by leveraging dialogue context and speaker interactions~\cite{shou2025comprehensivesurveymultimodalconversational}. Early sequence models using RNNs or GRUs capture short-term dependencies and speaker states~\cite{majumder2019dialoguernn,hazarika2018icon}, but often struggle with long-range interactions. 

Multimodal ERC (MERC) incorporates text, acoustic, and visual cues to better reflect human emotion expression~\cite{poria2019meld,zadeh2018multimodal}. Text provides lexical and semantic content, acoustic features convey speech patterns~\cite{schuller2011recognising}, and visual data encodes facial expressions and gestures~\cite{li2018self}. MERC introduces additional challenges, including modality alignment, noise handling, and effective cross-modal fusion~\cite{shou2025comprehensivesurveymultimodalconversational}.
\subsection{Approaches to MERC}

To address these challenges, several modeling strategies have been proposed.

\subsubsection{Sequence and Context Based Methods.}
Sequence models capture temporal dependencies and speaker states in conversations. DialogueRNN~\cite{majumder2019dialoguernn} uses recurrent structures to leverage dialog history for emotion prediction but has limited ability to model complex speaker or cross-modal interactions.
\subsubsection{Fusion Based Methods.}
Fusion approaches integrate multimodal information using different strategies. Early and late fusion methods combine modality features either at the input level or at the decision stage and are widely used baselines in multimodal sentiment and emotion analysis~\cite{morency2011multimodal,perezrosas2013utterance,poria2016fusing,zadeh2016mosi}. More advanced approaches employ attention mechanisms to model interactions between modalities. Multimodal Transformer architectures use cross modal attention to align textual, acoustic, and visual representations~\cite{tsai2019mult}, while MISA learns modality invariant and modality specific representations for improved multimodal integration~\cite{hazarika2020misa}. Additionally, models such as DialogueRNN and related methods incorporate mechanisms to track speaker states and emotion transitions within conversations~\cite{majumder2019dialoguernn,hazarika2018icon}. 

Although fusion methods improve multimodal interaction, they often do not explicitly represent conversational structure or relational dependencies between speakers.
\subsection{Graph Based MERC Approaches}

Graph based methods are powerful for MERC as they encode conversational structure and relational dependencies~\cite{hu2021mmgcn,li2024graphsmile}. Utterances are nodes and edges represent speaker interactions, temporal continuity, and cross-modal links~\cite{kipf2017gcn}. DialogueGCN~\cite{ghosal2019dialoguegcn} introduced graph reasoning for ERC, modeling intra- and inter-speaker relations and outperforming sequential models. Extensions like MMGCN~\cite{hu2021mmgcn} and hypergraph models capture multimodal interactions, while \textbf{Joyful}~\cite{li-etal-2023-joyful} combines graph fusion with contrastive learning to improve multimodal representations and address issues like oversmoothing.

\textbf{GraphSmile}~\cite{li2024graphsmile} represents a state-of-the-art graph-centric MERC architecture introducing two key components: \textit{Graph Structure Fusion (GSF)}~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, which constructs bipartite dialogue graphs~\cite{hu2021mmgcn} between every pair of modalities and alternates information propagation across these graphs to prevent any single structural path from dominating; and \textit{Sentiment Dynamics Perception (SDP)}~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, an auxiliary task that explicitly predicts whether sentiment shifts occur between consecutive utterances to guide the model toward emotional transitions.
Despite its strong performance, GraphSmile, like most existing graph based MERC methods depends on conventional visual encoders (\eg, CNN backbones~\cite{he2016resnet} or handcrafted facial features~\cite{baltrusaitis2018openface}) that produce static, low level visual embeddings. These embeddings are typically mean-pooled over frames, collapsing intra utterance temporal dynamics and limiting the visual modality's contribution to emotional reasoning. Graph based approaches excel at structurally modeling dialogues and multimodal interactions, but their effectiveness is often constrained by the quality of unimodal representations feeding into the graph.

\subsection{Vision Language and LLM Based Approaches for Emotion Recognition}

To overcome limitations of low level visual features, recent research has explored Vision Language Models (VLMs) and multimodal Large Language Models (LLMs) for emotion recognition tasks. VLMs pretrained on large image/video text datasets~\cite{radford2021clip} produce semantically meaningful representations of visual content: they generate textual or semantic descriptions from visual inputs, capturing behaviors, expressions, and dynamics that are difficult for CNN~\cite{he2016resnet} or ViT~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} features to represent, and these descriptions are inherently aligned with language modalities~\cite{devlin2019bert}, facilitating cross modal fusion and multimodal reasoning.

Recent works demonstrate that VLMs can capture emotionally relevant visual cues. Models such as VidEmo-3B~\cite{videmo} leverage video--language pretraining to describe visual affective cues over time, producing semantically grounded visual narratives. Task specific adaptations like Emotion-Qwen~\cite{huang2025emotionqwen} build hybrid frameworks that integrate emotion reasoning into general vision--language understanding by combining specialized emotion experts with general VL backbones. Evaluation studies of general VLMs show that, with appropriate prompting or fine tuning, vision--language models can recognize nuanced emotional expressions and behaviors that traditional encoders miss.

Multimodal LLM integrations extend this idea further. Works like BeMERC~\cite{fu2025bemerc} propose behavior aware MLLM frameworks for MERC, using multimodal LLMs tuned to behavior features (\eg, facial motion, body language) to directly predict conversational emotions, showing that LLMs can leverage both visual and linguistic context effectively. Prompt based or zero shot methods demonstrate that general large vision language systems such as CLIP~\cite{radford2021clip} or Qwen2.5-VL~\cite{qwen2_5_vl} can be prompted to interpret emotional cues in video or images without explicit retraining, offering flexible emotion inference.

These VLM/LLM approaches address two key limitations in traditional MERC: visual modality underutilization, by generating high level semantic visual descriptions rather than low level features~\cite{li2018self}; and cross modal alignment challenges, by inherently aligning visual and language modalities through shared semantic outputs~\cite{radford2021clip}. Integrating semantic VLM representations into structured MERC frameworks, especially graph based methods, emerges as an important frontier for multimodal emotion recognition.

\subsection{Positioning of Our Work}

Our work builds upon the strengths of graph based MERC frameworks , particularly GraphSmile~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, while addressing the underutilization of visual information. Instead of using static, low level visual embeddings~\cite{he2016resnet,baltrusaitis2018openface}, we introduce a VLM based semantic visual preprocessing stage~\cite{videmo,qwen2_5_vl} that converts raw video segments into temporally structured textual narratives capturing facial expressions, gaze shifts, and body language evolution within an utterance.

These semantic visual descriptions are encoded into dense vectors~\cite{devlin2019bert} and integrated into the Graph Structure Fusion (GSF) pipeline~\cite{li2024graphsmile}. This design preserves intra utterance temporal dynamics, improves cross modal alignment, reduces reliance on noisy visual appearance features, and enhances the model's ability to leverage visual cues in relational reasoning across conversation turns. By combining high level semantic visual reasoning from VLMs with structured graph propagation, our approach advances MERC beyond existing graph based and VLM based methods.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

The proposed framework builds upon the GraphSmile architecture~\cite{li2024graphsmile} by introducing a novel approach that converts raw visual inputs into meaningful semantic representations before multimodal fusion.

While prior graph based Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversation (MERC) systems relied on conventional visual encoders (such as DenseNet for extracting abstract spatial features~\cite{huang2017densely} and OpenFace for tracking deterministic facial mechanics like Action Units~\cite{baltrusaitis2018openface}), our methodology moves beyond these restricted appearance descriptors. By leveraging a temporally aware Vision Language Model (VLM)~\cite{radford2021clip,qwen2_5_vl}, we encode the rich, semantically grounded behavioral signals present in human speech.

The overall architecture comprises three main stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The independent extraction of utterance level features across textual, acoustic, and visual modalities.
  \item The construction of multimodal dialogue graphs for alternating propagation via the Graph Structure Fusion (GSF) module~\cite{li2024graphsmile}.
  \item Multitask classification incorporating a Sentiment Dynamics Perception (SDP) module~\cite{li2024graphsmile}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Utterance Level Feature Extraction}

Each modality is processed independently at the utterance level before being integrated into the multimodal graph.
In contrast to the visual channel, the textual and acoustic modality remains structurally unchanged relative to the GraphSmile baseline~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, preserving a controlled point of comparison.

\subsubsection{Textual Feature Extraction.}
For the textual modality, utterance transcripts are encoded uniformly across both datasets using a pretrained transformer language model, specifically RoBERTa~\cite{liu2019roberta}. The \texttt{<s>} (classification) token representation of each transcript forms the textual feature vector, $X^t$, capturing lexical and syntactic sentiment signals.

\subsubsection{Acoustic Feature Extraction.}
The acoustic features, $X^a$, capture paralinguistic speech characteristics including fundamental frequency (F0), energy, spectral properties, and temporal statistical functionals that reflect prosody and pitch dynamics. For both the IEMOCAP (6-way)~\cite{busso2008iemocap} and MELD~\cite{poria2019meld} datasets, these utterance level features are extracted using the openSMILE toolkit with the IS10 configuration~\cite{eyben2010opensmile}.

\subsubsection{Semantic Visual Feature Extraction.}
\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Preprocessing.pdf}
  \caption{The multimodal preprocessing pipeline for extracting utterance-level textual, acoustic, and visual features.}
  \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}
Traditional visual encoders often fail to fully harness conversational cues because they are restricted to extracting context blind spatial features or isolated facial mechanics, creating a semantic gap between raw physical movements and complex emotional states. The core methodological contribution addresses this by redefining the visual feature extraction process through a three stage semantic preprocessing pipeline.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 1: Dataset Specific Video Preprocessing.}
To ensure the VLM accurately analyzes the correct subject, we apply tailored preprocessing strategies based on the native format of each dataset. The IEMOCAP dataset~\cite{busso2008iemocap}, which consists of continuous, multi speaker dialogue recordings, undergoes two explicit preprocessing steps. First, the sessions are temporally segmented into discrete, utterance level clips using provided timestamp annotations; second, each frame is spatially cropped to physically isolate the active speaker and eliminate visual interference. In contrast, the MELD dataset~\cite{poria2019meld} inherently provides presegmented utterance level clips, bypassing the need for physical cropping. Instead, spatial isolation for MELD is achieved entirely through zero shot prompting, wherein the VLM is explicitly instructed to autonomously identify and focus exclusively on the active speaker within the uncropped, multi person frame.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 2: VLM Temporal Analysis.}
Each utterance clip is processed by VidEmo-3B~\cite{videmo} (built on the Qwen2.5-VL-3B backbone~\cite{qwen2_5_vl}). The model is driven by a highly structured prompt instructing it to focus exclusively on the active speaker and ignore any other visible participants. The VLM maps the speaker's emotional evolution by dividing the clip into a temporal progression (beginning, middle, and end). For each segment, the model grounds its analysis in specific visible signals across four dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Facial Expressions:} Specific muscle movements of the brow, lips, eyes, jaw, and cheeks.
  \item \textbf{Head \& Gaze:} Tilts, nods, shakes, and eye direction.
  \item \textbf{Body Language:} Posture shifts, gestures, and physical tension or relaxation.
  \item \textbf{Instantaneous Emotion:} The most likely emotional state, explicitly supported by the aforementioned visual cues.
\end{itemize}
The temporal analysis concludes with a one sentence summary of the overall emotional arc (\eg, \emph{starts neutral $\rightarrow$ builds frustration $\rightarrow$ brief smile at end}). By enforcing that every observation is grounded in a specific visible signal from the speaker, this stage generates a semantically dense, temporally aware description of affective behavior, bypassing the noise of traditional frame averaging encoders.

In a separate experimental condition on the MELD dataset, BLIP-2~\cite{li2023blip2} is employed as an alternative visual analysis model within this stage. Rather than processing the full video clip, three representative frames are sampled at fixed temporal positions corresponding to the beginning, middle, and end of each utterance. Each frame is individually processed by BLIP-2 with a prompt focused exclusively on the emotional state of the speaker in that moment, and the resulting per-frame descriptions are concatenated into a single utterance-level textual representation. This frame-level approach produces more targeted emotional descriptions, reducing scene-level noise that is particularly prevalent in the multi-party MELD setting.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 3: Text Encoding of VLM Output.}
To bridge the symbolic output of the VLM with the continuous feature space expected by the graph network, the free text description is encoded using a pretrained transformer language model. Two encoders are evaluated in separate experimental conditions: BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}, which truncates inputs to 512 tokens and extracts the \texttt{[CLS]} token embedding; and Longformer~\cite{beltagy2020longformer} , which processes the full description without truncation. Both produce a 768-dimensional dense vector that serves as the new visual modality feature, $X^v$. Encoding visual behavior in the same semantic space as the transcript fosters superior cross modal alignment within the GSF module~\cite{li2024graphsmile}.

\subsection{Graph Structure Fusion (GSF) Module}

The Graph Structure Fusion (GSF) module processes dialogue graphs layer by layer, alternating between sequential edges for temporal flow and speaker edges for personal consistency. This back-and-forth approach prevents any single relationship from dominating and effectively separates a speaker's internal emotional shifts from the conversation's external influences.
\subsubsection{Multimodal Dialogue Graphs}
To understand how utterances relate to each other across different sensory inputs, we build three bipartite dialogue graphs~\cite{hu2021mmgcn,li2024graphsmile}. Each graph connects two specific modalities. For example, the textual visual graph is defined as $\mathcal{G}^{tv} = (\mathcal{N}^t, \mathcal{N}^v, \mathcal{E}^{t \leftarrow v}, \mathcal{E}^{v \leftarrow t})$. Here, $\mathcal{N}^t$ and $\mathcal{N}^v$ stand for the sets of textual and visual nodes. The edge sets $\mathcal{E}^{t \leftarrow v}$ and $\mathcal{E}^{v \leftarrow t}$ represent the connections directed from visual to textual nodes, and vice versa.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Nodes:} Every utterance $u_i$ is represented by a textual node $n^t_i$ and a visual node $n^v_i$.
  \item \textbf{Edges:} We only draw edges between nodes of opposite modalities. To keep the graph from becoming too dense, we use a sliding window approach. A textual node $n^t_i$ connects to visual nodes from the past $P$ steps (i.e., $n^v_{i-P}, \dots, n^v_{i-1}$) and the future $F$ steps (i.e., $n^v_{i+1}, \dots, n^v_{i+F}$).
  \item \textbf{Edge Weights:} These connections are directed to show the specific flow of information. We assign trainable weights to these edges, which start at a value of 1 and are fine tuned as the model trains.
  \item \textbf{Adjacency Matrix:} All of these node and edge relationships are stored in an adjacency matrix. This matrix is the core structural input for the graph neural network.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Alternate Graph Propagation}
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) \cite{kipf2017gcn,scarselli2008graph} are highly effective at uncovering both cross modal and intra modal relationships. In our design, each dialogue graph passes information through multiple simplified convolutional layers. It alternates between extracting inter modal and intra modal features layer by layer, calculated as:

$$X^{(l)} = AX^{(l-1)}\Theta^{(l)}, \quad l = 1, 2, \dots, L$$

Here, $A$ is the dialogue graph's adjacency matrix, $X^{(l)}$ is the output feature at layer $l$, and $\Theta^{(l)}$ is the trainable weight matrix for that layer. For the textual-visual graph, the starting input $X^{tv,(0)}$ simply combines the raw textual features $[x^t_1, \dots, x^t_M]$ and visual features $[x^v_1, \dots, x^v_M]$.

Next, we use residual connections \cite{he2016resnet} to sum the feature representations from every layer:

$$X = X^{(0)} + \text{FC}^{(1)}(X^{(1)}) + \dots + \text{FC}^{(L)}(X^{(L)})$$

The fully connected layer $\text{FC}(\cdot)$ includes a linear transformation, a LeakyReLU \cite{maas2013rectifier} activation, and dropout \cite{srivastava2014dropout}:

$$\text{FC}(X) = \text{Dropout}(\text{LeakyReLU}(X\Theta_f + b_f))$$

This setup serves two purposes: it lets the network dynamically choose which layer's information is most useful, and it boosts the overall expressive power of the GSF module.

\subsubsection{Multimodal Integration}
After processing the textual-visual ($\mathcal{G}^{tv}$), textual-acoustic ($\mathcal{G}^{ta}$), and visual-acoustic ($\mathcal{G}^{va}$) graphs through their own separate GSF modules, we get three distinct outputs: $X^{tv}$, $X^{ta}$, and $X^{va}$. We add these together to create a final, unified feature representation:

\begin{align}
  H =\; & \sigma\!\left((X^{t \leftarrow v})\Theta_h\right) + \sigma\!\left((X^{v \leftarrow t})\Theta_h\right) \nonumber   \\
        & + \sigma\!\left((X^{t \leftarrow a})\Theta_h\right) + \sigma\!\left((X^{a \leftarrow t})\Theta_h\right) \nonumber \\
        & + \sigma\!\left((X^{v \leftarrow a})\Theta_h\right) + \sigma\!\left((X^{a \leftarrow v})\Theta_h\right)
\end{align}

In this equation, $\sigma$ is the activation function (like LeakyReLU) and $\Theta_h$ is a shared learning parameter. This combined matrix $H$ effectively captures all the complex, multi sensory emotional signals in the conversation.

\subsection{Emotion Classification}

After the graph propagation and integration phase, the resulting feature matrix $H$ is passed into a multitask classification head.
For the primary task of emotion classification, $H$ goes through a linear classifier and a softmax function to predict the exact emotional category. This is formulated as:

$$\hat{Y} = \text{softmax}(H\Theta_y + b_y)$$
$$\hat{e}_i = \text{argmax}(\hat{y}_i[\tau])$$

Here, $\Theta_y$ and $b_y$ are the learned weights and biases. The vector $\hat{y}_i$ holds the emotion probabilities for utterance $u_i$, and $\hat{e}_i$ is the final predicted emotion label. We optimize the network using categorical cross-entropy loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_e = -\frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \sum_{j=1}^{C_e} y_{i,j} \log \hat{y}_{i,j}$$

where $M$ is the total number of utterances, $C_e$ is the number of emotion classes, $y_{i,j}$ is the true label, and $\hat{y}_{i,j}$ is our model's prediction.

\subsection{Sentiment Dynamics Perception (SDP) Module}
Existing dialogue models often focus on static context, missing crucial inter utterance sentiment changes. To address this, we introduce the Sentiment Dynamics Perception (SDP) module \cite{li2024graphsmile} as a vital auxiliary task. The SDP module frames conversation dynamics as a pairwise prediction problem, tracking whether sentiment shifts or remains constant between consecutive utterances. By jointly optimizing this auxiliary shift loss with the main emotion classification loss, the network is strictly guided to learn the dialogue's emotional momentum. This joint supervision improves class separation (pulling similar sentiments closer and pushing differing ones apart), while perfectly aligning with the temporal descriptions created during the VLM preprocessing stage.
\subsubsection{Shift Label}
We define a sentiment shift label $o_{(i,j)}$ for any pair of utterances $u_i$ and $u_j$. If the sentiment changes between the two utterances, the label is 1; if the sentiment remains the same, the label is 0:
$$
  o_{(i,j)} =
  \begin{cases}
    1, & \text{if } s_i \neq s_j, \\
    0, & \text{if } s_i = s_j.
  \end{cases}
$$
where $s_i$ denotes the ground truth sentiment state of utterance $u_i$.

\subsubsection{Shift Classification}
We construct pairwise shift features $T$ from the fused representations and pass them through a softmax classifier to predict the shift probability $\hat{Z}$:
$$
  \hat{Z} = \text{softmax}(T\Theta_z + b_z)
$$
where $\Theta_z$ and $b_z$ are learnable parameters. The module is optimized using a standard cross-entropy loss function, denoted as $\mathcal{L}_o$.

\subsection{Sentiment Classification}
To extend our model to the Multimodal Sentiment Analysis in Conversation (MSAC) task, we add a sentiment classifier. The fused feature matrix $H$ is processed through a linear layer and a softmax activation:
$$
  \hat{W} = \text{softmax}(H\Theta_w + b_w)
$$
Here, $\Theta_w$ and $b_w$ are learnable parameters, and $\hat{W}$ contains the predicted sentiment probabilities. This task is supervised by a sentiment classification cross-entropy loss, denoted as $\mathcal{L}_s$.

\subsection{Total Training Objective}
To simultaneously extract both emotional and sentimental cues, the primary emotion recognition task is jointly learned alongside the sentiment and shift classification tasks. The overall network parameters are optimized using a combined loss function:
$$
  \mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_e + \lambda_s \mathcal{L}_s + \lambda_o \mathcal{L}_o + \beta ||\Theta_{total}||
$$
where $\mathcal{L}_e$ is the primary emotion classification loss, $\lambda_s$ and $\lambda_o$ are trade-off hyperparameters balancing the auxiliary tasks, $\beta$ is the $L_2$ regularization weight, and $\Theta_{total}$ represents all trainable parameters in the model.

\section{Experimental Details}

\subsection{Datasets Overview}

\noindent\textbf{IEMOCAP}~\cite{busso2008iemocap}\textbf{.} The dataset consists of continuous, dyadic conversational sessions recorded with a dual-camera setup, presenting both actors side-by-side in a single unsegmented video frame. The accompanying audio is recorded in stereo, capturing each speaker on a dedicated independent microphone channel.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{MELD}~\cite{poria2019meld}\textbf{.} This dataset is constructed from episodes of the television series \textit{Friends}, representing a multi-party conversational environment. The video data is inherently provided as pre-segmented, utterance-level clips.

\subsection{Utterance-Level Video Preparation}

\noindent\textbf{IEMOCAP.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Segment each conversation into utterance level clips using timestamps.
  \item Crop frames to isolate the active speaker.
  \item Retain only the speaker's audio channel from the stereo stream.
  \item Output speaker focused utterance level video clips.
\end{itemize}

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{MELD.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Pre-segmented utterance level clips; no cropping needed.
  \item Each clip corresponds to a single speaker utterance.
  \item Speaker focus is handled during prompting.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Semantic Visual Feature Extraction}

\noindent\textbf{Video-to-Text Generation.}
Vision Language Models (VLMs) are utilized to generate descriptive text for each utterance. The VidEmo-3B model~\cite{videmo}, built upon the Qwen2.5-VL backbone~\cite{qwen2_5_vl}, is applied to both datasets.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Frame Sampling.} IEMOCAP videos are sampled at a fixed rate of 1 frame per second (FPS). MELD videos use an adaptive strategy of 1 FPS for clips under 15 seconds, capped at a maximum of 16 frames for longer clips.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Prompt Structure.} The prompt requests a temporal progression (beginning, middle, and end) detailing facial expressions, head and gaze, body language, and instantaneous emotion, concluding with a one-sentence emotional arc summary. For MELD, zero-shot instructions are included to focus exclusively on the active speaker.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Batching and Computation.} For IEMOCAP, inference was executed with a batch size of 4, requiring over 7 hours. For MELD, inference was executed with a batch size of 2, requiring over 10 hours.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Text-to-Vector Encoding.}
The generated descriptions are encoded using two models evaluated in separate experimental conditions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Longformer}~\cite{beltagy2020longformer} : inputs are processed without truncation; a 768-dimensional embedding is extracted from the \texttt{[CLS]} token.
  \item \textbf{BERT}~\cite{devlin2019bert} : inputs are truncated to a maximum of 512 tokens; a 768-dimensional embedding is extracted from the \texttt{[CLS]} token.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Hardware and Environment.}
All experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060 (12\,GB VRAM) using a Conda environment with PyTorch 2.8.0+cu126.

\subsection{Integration into Graph Based MERC Framework}

\noindent\textbf{Feature Substitution and Dimensionality.}
The 768-dimensional visual embeddings generated via the VLM and subsequent text encoding are substituted for the original 342-dimensional handcrafted visual descriptors in the dataset manifest. A linear projection layer is implemented at the input of the visual processing stream to map the 768-dimensional vectors into the hidden dimension space of the graph architecture. Textual and acoustic features remain identical to the GraphSmile baseline~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, with dimensions of 1024 (RoBERTa) and 1582 (openSMILE IS10) for IEMOCAP, and 1024 and 300 respectively for MELD.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
Accuracy (ACC) and Weighted F1-score (WF1) serve as the primary evaluation metrics for overall model performance. The F1-score is further utilized to evaluate classification performance for each individual emotion category.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Training and Optimization Strategy.}
The model follows the multi-task optimization strategy of GraphSmile~\cite{li2024graphsmile}, jointly learning the primary emotion recognition task alongside sentiment and sentiment-shift classification. All structural components of the Graph Structure Fusion (GSF) module and the Sentiment Dynamics Perception (SDP) module are maintained as per the original implementation to ensure a controlled comparison of visual feature effectiveness.

On the IEMOCAP dataset, GraphSmile with BERT-based encoding of VLM-generated descriptions achieved the highest performance, with a weighted F1 score of 71.65, slightly improving over the baseline GraphSmile (WF1 71.29). Qwen-VL-3B with BERT also slightly outperformed Longformer-based encoding (ACC 67.32 vs.\ 67.13; WF1 66.12 vs.\ 65.97). \subsection{Observations}

The results demonstrate that semantic visual representations can effectively replace traditional handcrafted visual descriptors in graph-based MERC frameworks without significant loss in performance. In some cases, modest improvements were observed, particularly on IEMOCAP where the target speaker could be isolated.

The choice of text encoder affects performance depending on the length of VLM-generated descriptions. While Longformer can handle sequences up to 4096 tokens, most descriptions were within the 512-token limit of BERT. Consequently, BERT encoded the majority of samples efficiently without excessive padding, yielding slightly better performance than Longformer.

Dataset characteristics also influence the effectiveness of semantic visual features. IEMOCAP clips have relatively low spatial resolution ($360\times480$ after speaker-focused splitting) and short durations, limiting the visibility of fine-grained facial expressions and the temporal context available for emotional inference. Additionally, VLM outputs occasionally contain hallucinated or repetitive descriptions, introducing noise into the semantic embeddings.

On MELD, multi-party interactions complicate speaker-specific emotion recognition. Multiple characters appearing simultaneously make it difficult for the VLM to isolate the target speaker, often resulting in descriptions that focus on overall scene context rather than the emotional state of the intended individual. Hallucinations and inconsistent outputs, sometimes exacerbated by larger batch sizes during generation, add further noise to the embeddings.

Frame-level analysis with BLIP-2~\cite{li2023blip2}, where three representative frames from each clip (beginning, middle, end) were used, yielded more focused descriptions centered on the speaker's emotional state. This approach reduced redundancy and improved performance slightly compared to full-scene VLM descriptions, suggesting that targeted visual reasoning on key frames can be advantageous, particularly in multi-party settings.

Overall, these observations highlight that semantic visual representations are a viable alternative to handcrafted features in MERC, but their effectiveness is highly dependent on scene structure, speaker isolation, and quality of VLM outputs. Future improvements may be achieved through explicit speaker localization, noise reduction in VLM descriptions, and optimized frame sampling strategies for temporal emotion tracking.

\section{Conclusion}

This work explores an alternative approach to representing visual information in graph-based Multimodal Emotion Recognition in Conversations (MERC). Instead of relying on conventional pixel-level visual embeddings, we employ a Vision--Language Model (VLM) to transform utterance-level video segments into structured semantic descriptions that capture observable behavioral cues and their temporal progression.

These semantically grounded representations provide a different perspective on incorporating visual signals within conversational reasoning frameworks and demonstrate that visual information can be expressed in a form closer to how emotions are typically interpreted. Our findings suggest that such semantic visual representations can serve as a viable alternative to traditional visual feature pipelines used in graph-based MERC systems.

Future work may extend this idea to the acoustic modality by transforming speech signals into descriptive semantic representations that capture emotionally relevant characteristics of the audio. Additionally, architectures specifically designed to process semantic multimodal representations could further improve how such information is incorporated into conversational emotion recognition systems.
